# 03. NDA and Confidentiality Documents

## Purpose

Defines when and how to use confidentiality agreements before disclosing sensitive business information.

## NDA Types and Use Cases

## 1) Mutual NDA
- Use when both parties share confidential information.
- Typical scenarios:
  - Partner discussions
  - Brand collaborations
  - Strategic vendor negotiations

## 2) One-Way NDA
- Use when only your company discloses sensitive info.
- Typical scenarios:
  - Freelancer/designer onboarding
  - Manufacturing discussions
  - Agency discovery calls

## 3) Employee/Contractor Confidentiality Agreement
- Use with all team members handling sensitive data.
- Often combined with IP assignment and work-for-hire language.

## Required NDA Clauses
- Definition of confidential information
- Permitted use and restrictions
- Disclosure limitations and need-to-know handling
- Security and safeguarding obligations
- Exclusions (public, prior known, independently developed, legally compelled)
- Duration of confidentiality and survival period
- Return/destruction obligations on request
- Breach remedies and injunctive relief
- Governing law and venue

## Optional Clauses (Context-Dependent)
- Non-circumvention
- Non-solicitation (state-law sensitive)
- No license/no assignment of IP by disclosure
- Audit/access obligations for high-risk data

## Practical Workflow
1. Classify information to be disclosed (low/medium/high sensitivity).
2. Pick NDA type (mutual vs one-way).
3. Use approved template version.
4. Capture signatures before disclosure.
5. Share data through controlled channels only.
6. Maintain an NDA log with dates and counterparties.

## NDA Log Template Fields
- Counterparty legal name
- Contact person
- NDA type
- Effective date
- Expiration/end date
- Return/destruction status
- Linked project/opportunity

## Common Mistakes
- Sharing pitch decks with sensitive cost/vendor terms before signed NDA.
- Using verbal NDAs with no written agreement.
- Failing to track NDA expiration and survival obligations.
- Using broad non-compete language that may be unenforceable.
